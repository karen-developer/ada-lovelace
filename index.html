Ada Lovelace

The First Computer Programmer

1815 - 1852

"Mathematical science shows what is. It is the language of unseen relations between things. But to use and apply that language, we must be able fully to appreciate, to feel, to seize the unseen, the unconscious." -- Ada Lovelace

Pioneer of Programming

Lady Ada, the daughter of British poet Lord Byron, was a 19th century woman of many talents but with a particular leaning towards mathematics. She is commonly regarded as the world’s first programmer based on her work with Charles Babbage on his Analytical Engine. Her choice to study and specialize in the science, technology, engineering and mathematics (STEM) disciplines was highly exceptional for a woman in 19th century England.

Her paper is still considered one of her greatest contributions to computer science, distinguishing it from the science of mathematics. One of her notes within the piece included a method for calculating a sequence of Bernoulli numbers. Known as "Note G," it is considered the first computer program in history.

Timeline of Ada's life

  • 1815: Born to parents George Gordon Byron and Anne Isabella Milbanke
  • 1816: Ada and her mother move to live with her grandparents at Kirkby Mallory
  • 1824: Ada's father, poet Lord Byron, dies in Greece
  • 1828: Ada starts constructing wings so that she could fly
  • 1829: Ada is paralysed after a bout of measles
  • 1831: Ada starts walking with crutches
  • 1833: Introduced to Charles Babbage
  • 1835: Marries William, 8th Baron King
  • 1836: Her first son, Byron, is born
  • 1837: Her daughter, Anne Isabella, is born
  • 1839: Her youngest son, Ralph Gordon, is born
  • 1843: Ada's translation and Notes on the analytical machine are published
  • 1852: Ada died at the age of 36
